2Ô∏è‚É£ Significant Figures

Significant figures are the digits in a number that carry meaningful information about its precision. They tell us how precise our measurements are.

Rules for Identifying Significant Figures:

  1. All non-zero digits are significant (1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8, 9)
  2. Zeros between non-zero digits are significant
  3. Leading zeros are NOT significant (zeros before the first non-zero digit)
  4. Trailing zeros after a decimal point are significant
  5. Trailing zeros in whole numbers without decimals are ambiguous (use scientific notation to clarify)

Examples with Explanations:

  • 6753 has 4 significant figures (all non-zero)
  • 40072 has 5 significant figures (zeros between non-zero digits count)
  • 0.0089 has 2 significant figures (leading zeros don't count)
  • 9.0 has 2 significant figures (trailing zero after decimal counts)
  • 0.00450 has 3 significant figures (4, 5, and trailing 0)
  • 100.00 has 5 significant figures (all zeros after decimal count)
  • 0.05060 has 4 significant figures (5, 0, 6, and trailing 0)
  • 3005 has 4 significant figures (zero between non-zero digits counts)
  • 0.000708 has 3 significant figures (7, 0, 8)
  • 82.000 has 5 significant figures (trailing zeros after decimal count)
  • 500 has 1 significant figure (ambiguous; could be 1, 2, or 3)
  • 5.00 √ó 10¬≤ has 3 significant figures (scientific notation clarifies)

Rounding to Significant Figures:

Rule: Look at the digit after your last significant figure

  • If it's 5 or more ‚Üí round up
  • If it's 4 or less ‚Üí round down

Examples:

  • 14.628 ‚Üí 14.63 (4 sig figs) ‚Äî the 8 causes rounding up
  • 15.473 ‚Üí 15.47 (4 sig figs) ‚Äî the 3 means round down
  • 0.006789 ‚Üí 0.0068 (2 sig figs) ‚Äî the 8 causes rounding up

  • 9.9999 ‚Üí 10.0 (3 sig figs) ‚Äî rounds up to next whole number

  • 3.14159 ‚Üí 3.14 (3 sig figs)
  • 8765.4 ‚Üí 8800 (2 sig figs)

3Ô∏è‚É£ Expressing Results with Significant Figures

When performing calculations, the result should reflect the appropriate precision based on the input values.

Addition and Subtraction:

Rule: Round to the least number of decimal places

Examples:

  • 2345 + 7800 + 934,456 = 944,601
    7800 has the least precision (to the hundreds place)
    Answer: 940,000 (2 sig figs)
  • 12.11 + 18.0 + 1.013 = 31.123

    Answer: 31.1
  • 156.8 - 0.56 = 156.24

    Answer: 156.2
  • 0.0045 + 0.023 + 0.1 = 0.1275
    0.1 has only 1 decimal place

4Ô∏è‚É£ Accuracy vs. Precision

These two concepts are often confused, but they describe different aspects of measurement quality.

üéØ Accuracy

How close a measurement is to the true or accepted value.

Example:

True mass of object = 30.0g

Your measurement = 29.9g

‚úì This is ACCURATE

üìç Precision

How close repeated measurements are to each other.

Example:

Measurement 1 = 30.01g

Measurement 2 = 30.02g

Measurement 3 = 30.03g

‚úì This is PRECISE

Detailed Examples:

Example 1: Both Accurate and Precise

True value: 30.0g

Measurements: 30.01g, 30.02g, 30.03g

‚úì Accurate (close to 30.0g) AND Precise (close to each other)

Example 2: Precise but Not Accurate

True value: 30.0g

Measurements: 35.21g, 35.23g, 35.22g

‚ö† Precise (consistent) but NOT Accurate (far from true value)

Example 3: Accurate but Not Precise

True value: 30.0g

Measurements: 28.5g, 31.2g, 29.8g, 30.5g

‚ö† Average is Accurate (‚âà30.0g) but NOT Precise (values scattered)

Example 4: Neither Accurate nor Precise

True value: 30.0g

Measurements: 25.1g, 32.8g, 27.3g, 34.5g

‚úó NOT Accurate (far from true value) AND NOT Precise (scattered)

üéì Key Takeaway:

In chemistry, we want measurements that are BOTH accurate and precise. Precision alone isn't enough if your instrument has a systematic error!
