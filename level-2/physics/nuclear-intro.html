Nuclear physics deals with the study of the nucleus of an atom. It explains the structure of atomic nuclei, isotopes, radioactive emissions, and the uses and dangers of radioactivity in everyday life.

‚öõÔ∏è Nuclear Structure of an Atom

An atom consists of a central nucleus surrounded by electrons. The nucleus contains protons and neutrons, known as nucleons.

  • Protons have positive charge.
  • Neutrons have no charge.
  • Electrons move around the nucleus.

Atomic nuclei are represented using nuclear notation, for example: 2311Na, where 23 is the mass number and 11 is the atomic number.

üß¨ Isotopes

Isotopes are atoms of the same element that have the same number of protons but different numbers of neutrons.

  • Same atomic number
  • Different mass numbers

Examples include hydrogen-1, hydrogen-2, and hydrogen-3. Some isotopes are unstable and are known as radioactive isotopes.

‚ò¢Ô∏è Types of Radioactive Emissions

Radioactive emissions are released from unstable nuclei. There are three main types:

  • Alpha (Œ±): Positively charged, weak penetration.
  • Beta (Œ≤): Negatively charged, moderate penetration.
  • Gamma (Œ≥): No charge, very high penetration.

üìä Properties of Nuclear Radiations

  • Alpha particles are stopped by paper.
  • Beta particles are stopped by aluminium.
  • Gamma rays require thick lead or concrete.
  • All radiations can ionise matter.

‚ö†Ô∏è Dangers and Safety Measures

Radioactive emissions can damage body cells and cause cancer or genetic mutations.

  • Use shielding such as lead containers.
  • Reduce exposure time.
  • Store radioactive materials safely.
  • Use protective clothing and warning signs.

üåç Applications of Radioactivity

  • Nuclear power generation.
  • Medical treatment (radiotherapy).
  • Agricultural and biochemical tracers.
  • Industrial testing and thickness control.
  • Sterilisation and food preservation.
