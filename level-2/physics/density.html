Definition and Basic Concepts

Density is defined as the mass of a substance per unit volume. It is a derived quantity that depends on both mass and volume. Density tells us how tightly matter is packed together in a substance.

Anomalous Behavior of Water (4¬∞C to 0¬∞C)

Water exhibits unusual behavior when cooled from 4¬∞C to 0¬∞C, which is called anomalous expansion.

Normal Behavior (above 4¬∞C):

  • ‚Ä¢ Water contracts when cooled (volume decreases)
  • ‚Ä¢ Density increases as temperature drops

Anomalous Behavior (4¬∞C to 0¬∞C):

  • ‚Ä¢ Water expands when cooled (volume increases)
  • ‚Ä¢ Density decreases as temperature drops

At 4¬∞C: Water has its maximum density

At 0¬∞C: Ice forms and floats because it is less dense than water

Why Ice Floats:

Ice has a lower density than liquid water due to its crystalline structure, which creates more space between molecules.

Effects of Water's Unusual Expansion

1. Freezing of Lakes and Ponds

Process:

  1. When air temperature drops below 0¬∞C, the surface water cools first
  2. Water at 4¬∞C (highest density) sinks to the bottom
  3. Warmer water rises to the surface
  4. Ice forms at 0¬∞C on the surface and floats
  5. Water below the ice remains at approximately 4¬∞C

Survival of Aquatic Life:

Fish and other aquatic organisms survive because the water beneath the ice layer remains liquid at 4¬∞C, providing a livable environment throughout winter.

2. Bursting of Water Pipes

Cause:

When water inside pipes freezes, it expands significantly (approximately 9% volume increase). This expansion creates enormous pressure against the pipe walls.

Result:

The internal pressure can exceed the pipe's strength, causing it to crack or burst. This commonly occurs in cold climates when pipes are not properly insulated.

3. Weathering of Rocks

Process:

  1. Water seeps into cracks and crevices in rocks
  2. When temperature drops below 0¬∞C, the water freezes
  3. Freezing water expands, exerting pressure on the rock
  4. Repeated freeze-thaw cycles gradually widen the cracks
  5. Eventually, the rock breaks apart

Significance:

This is a major form of mechanical weathering that shapes landscapes over time, particularly in regions with frequent temperature fluctuations around 0¬∞C.

Relative Density

Definition:

Relative density (RD) is the ratio of the mass of a substance to the mass of an equal volume of water.

RD = Mass of substance / Mass of equal volume of water

RD = Density of substance / Density of water

Important Characteristic:

Relative density has NO UNITS because it is a ratio of two similar quantities.

Worked Example: Relative Density

Given:

  • Empty bottle mass = 20 g
  • Bottle filled with water = 40 g
  • Bottle filled with liquid X = 30 g

Step 1: Find mass of water

Mass of water = 40 g - 20 g = 20 g

Step 2: Find mass of liquid X

Mass of liquid X = 30 g - 20 g = 10 g

Step 3: Calculate relative density

RD = Mass of liquid X / Mass of water

RD = 10 g / 20 g

RD = 0.5

Interpretation:

Liquid X is half as dense as water

Average Density

Definition:

Average density is the ratio of the total mass of all materials in an object to the total volume occupied by that object, including any hollow spaces.

Average Density = Total Mass / Total Volume

Key Concept:

Average density accounts for air spaces and hollow regions within an object.

Experiment: Average Density of Iron

Apparatus:

  • Iron pin (solid)
  • Iron sheet/plate (hollow or shaped)
  • Container of water

Procedure:

  1. Place an iron pin in water
  2. Place an iron plate on the surface of water

Observations:

Iron pin sinks

Its density is greater than water (approximately 7,800 kg/m¬≥)

Iron plate floats

Its average density is less than water

Explanation:

Although both objects are made of iron, the plate contains air spaces or has a shape that traps air. This air reduces the average density of the plate-air system to below that of water, allowing it to float.

Average Density = (Mass of iron + Mass of air) / (Volume of iron + Volume of air)

Since air has very low mass but occupies significant volume, the average density decreases.

Density of Alloys

What are Alloys?

Alloys are mixtures of two or more metals combined to create materials with improved properties.

Key Property:

The density of an alloy lies between the densities of the pure metals used to make it.

Example:

Metal A density = 5,000 kg/m¬≥

Metal B density = 9,000 kg/m¬≥

Alloy A+B density = somewhere between 5,000 and 9,000 kg/m¬≥

Common Alloys:

  • Bronze = Copper + Tin
  • Brass = Copper + Zinc
  • Steel = Iron + Carbon

The exact density depends on the proportions of each metal in the mixture.

Floating and Sinking

Upthrust:

An object submerged in a fluid experiences an upward force called upthrust (or buoyant force).

Object sinks when:

Density of object > Density of fluid

Object floats when:

Density of object < Density of fluid

Object suspends when:

Density of object = Density of fluid

Effect of Fluid Density:

A denser fluid exerts greater upthrust. This is why it's easier to float in salt water (higher density) than fresh water.
