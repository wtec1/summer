Relief refers to the height and steepness of land that determines its shape. Understanding relief is essential for reading topographic maps and interpreting landscapes.

Hachures

Short lines showing slope direction; darker on steep slopes

Spot Heights

Small dots with numbers indicating altitude

Contour Lines (Most Important)

Lines joining places of same altitude; never cross; equal vertical intervals

Layer Tinting

Color shades for different heights (green=low, brown=intermediate, white=highest)

2Ô∏è‚É£ Contour Lines

Characteristics:

  • Form complete circles
  • Do not cross each other
  • Have equal vertical intervals
  • Never divide or split
  • Numbers increase with altitude

Types of Contours:

  • Index Contours: Thick, numbered lines
  • Intermediate Contours: Thin, unnumbered lines
  • Supplementary Contours: Dashed lines in flat terrain

Plateau

Mountain with flat top (table mountain)

Cliff

Vertical elevation change with flat top

Knoll/Conical Hill

Round mountain with concentric contours

4Ô∏è‚É£ Gradient

Gradient measures the steepness of a slope, expressed as a ratio or fraction.

Formula:

Gradient = Vertical Interval √∑ Horizontal Distance

  • Vertical Interval (VI): Difference in height between two contours
  • Horizontal Distance: Distance between two points on map (converted to real distance)

Steps to Calculate:

  1. Find vertical interval (highest - lowest contour)
  2. Measure horizontal distance using map scale
  3. Divide VI by horizontal distance
  4. Express as ratio (e.g., 1:30)
