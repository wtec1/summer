üìö Introduction

Chemical equations are like mathematical sentences that describe what happens during a chemical reaction. Just as you balance both sides of a mathematical equation, you must balance chemical equations to obey the Law of Conservation of Mass, which states that matter cannot be created or destroyed in a chemical reaction.

Why Balance Equations?

The number of atoms of each element must be the same on both sides of the equation. This ensures that no atoms are lost or gained during the reaction.

Parts of a Chemical Equation:

Reactants ‚Üí Products

  • Reactants: Substances that start the reaction (on the left side)
  • Products: Substances formed by the reaction (on the right side)
  • Arrow (‚Üí): Means "yields" or "produces"
  • Plus sign (+): Separates two or more reactants or products
  • Coefficients: Numbers placed before formulas to balance the equation

Example:

2H‚ÇÇ + O‚ÇÇ ‚Üí 2H‚ÇÇO

This reads: "Two molecules of hydrogen gas react with one molecule of oxygen gas to produce two molecules of water."

State Symbols:

  • (s) = solid
  • (l) = liquid
  • (g) = gas
  • (aq) = aqueous (dissolved in water)

Example: 2Na(s) + 2H‚ÇÇO(l) ‚Üí 2NaOH(aq) + H‚ÇÇ(g)

2Ô∏è‚É£ The Law of Conservation of Mass

The Law of Conservation of Mass states that in any chemical reaction, the total mass of reactants equals the total mass of products. This means atoms are neither created nor destroyed‚Äîthey are simply rearranged.

Key Principle:

The number of atoms of each element on the reactant side must equal the number of atoms of that element on the product side.

Example - Unbalanced vs Balanced:

‚ùå Unbalanced: H‚ÇÇ + O‚ÇÇ ‚Üí H‚ÇÇO

Left side: 2 H, 2 O | Right side: 2 H, 1 O (Not equal!)

‚úì Balanced: 2H‚ÇÇ + O‚ÇÇ ‚Üí 2H‚ÇÇO

Left side: 4 H, 2 O | Right side: 4 H, 2 O (Equal!)

3Ô∏è‚É£ Step-by-Step Method for Balancing Equations

Follow These Steps:

  1. Write the unbalanced equation with correct chemical formulas
  2. Count the atoms of each element on both sides
  3. Balance one element at a time by adding coefficients (never change subscripts!)
  4. Start with the most complex molecule or the element that appears in only one reactant and one product
  5. Balance hydrogen and oxygen last (they appear in many compounds)
  6. Check your work by counting atoms of each element on both sides
  7. Use the smallest whole-number coefficients possible

‚ö†Ô∏è IMPORTANT RULE:

NEVER change the subscripts in chemical formulas! Only add coefficients in front of the formulas. Changing subscripts changes the substance itself.

üí° Quick Tips for Success
